🏃 Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy an object has because it is moving. The word "kinetic" comes from a Greek word meaning motion. Any object that is moving — a car, a ball, even air — has kinetic energy!

The faster something moves, or the heavier it is, the more kinetic energy it has. Imagine a bowling ball rolling slowly versus a baseball flying at full speed — they both have kinetic energy, but in different amounts.

⚡ Fun Fact!

A car crash is kinetic energy being converted to other forms — sound, heat, and the energy that crumples the metal. Seatbelts and airbags slow you down gradually to protect you!

Formula: KE = ½ × mass × speed²

This means if you double your speed, your kinetic energy becomes 4 times larger! That's why speeding in a car is so dangerous.

🏔️ Potential Energy

Potential energy is stored energy — energy that has the potential (possibility) to do work. Think of it as energy that's waiting, ready to be released and turned into kinetic energy!

There are different types of potential energy. Gravitational potential energy depends on how high something is. A roller coaster at the top of a hill has lots of it — which it converts to speed on the way down!

Elastic potential energy is stored when you stretch or compress something — like a spring, a rubber band, or a bow and arrow.

⚡ Fun Fact!

Water behind a dam has massive gravitational potential energy. When released, it flows down and spins turbines to make electricity. That's hydroelectric power!

🔥 Thermal Energy

Thermal energy is heat energy. Everything is made of tiny particles called atoms and molecules. These particles are always moving — vibrating, bouncing, spinning. The more they move, the more thermal energy an object has!

Temperature measures how fast particles are moving on average. When you heat something up, you're giving its particles more energy, making them move faster.

Heat always flows from hot to cold. That's why ice melts in your hand — the warmth from your hand flows into the cold ice!

⚡ Fun Fact!

At absolute zero (−273°C), particles would stop moving completely. Nothing in the universe has ever been cooled to exactly absolute zero — it's the coldest possible temperature!

💡 Light Energy

Light energy, also called radiant energy, travels in waves through space. Unlike sound, light doesn't need anything to travel through — it can move through the vacuum of outer space!

Light travels at about 300,000 kilometers per second — the fastest speed in the universe. Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to reach Earth!

The light you can see (visible light) is just a small part of the electromagnetic spectrum, which also includes radio waves, microwaves, X-rays, and ultraviolet light.

⚡ Fun Fact!

When you see a star twinkling, you're seeing light that left that star hundreds or even thousands of years ago. You're literally looking back in time!

⚗️ Chemical Energy

Chemical energy is stored in the bonds between atoms in molecules. When a chemical reaction happens, bonds break and form, releasing or absorbing energy. When bonds break and energy is released, we get light, heat, or electricity!

Your body uses chemical energy from food. When you eat, your body breaks down molecules like glucose (sugar) and releases energy to power your muscles, brain, and every cell.

Batteries store chemical energy and convert it to electrical energy when connected to a circuit. Cars use the chemical energy in gasoline — burning it creates hot gases that push pistons to move the car.

⚡ Fun Fact!

The human body converts about 2,000–3,000 food calories per day into energy. If all that energy were released at once as heat, it would take over 3 hours to boil a pot of water!

⚡ Electrical Energy

Electrical energy comes from the movement of tiny particles called electrons. Electrons are part of atoms, and in some materials (like copper wire), they can flow freely from atom to atom — this flow of electrons is called electric current!

Electrical energy is incredibly useful because it's easy to transport through wires and convert into other forms of energy. Light bulbs convert it to light. Heaters convert it to heat. Speakers convert it to sound.

We generate electricity in power plants by spinning large magnets inside coils of wire — this is called electromagnetic induction. The spinning can be powered by steam (from coal, gas, or nuclear), wind, or water.

⚡ Fun Fact!

Lightning is a massive discharge of electrical energy. A single bolt of lightning can be 5 times hotter than the surface of the Sun, reaching 30,000 Kelvin!

☀️ Solar Energy

Solar energy comes from the Sun! The Sun produces energy through nuclear fusion — hydrogen atoms fuse together to form helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy as light and heat.

Solar panels (also called photovoltaic cells) use special materials called semiconductors to convert sunlight directly into electricity. When photons (light particles) hit the panel, they knock electrons loose, creating electric current!

Solar energy is renewable — meaning it won't run out (at least not for another 5 billion years when the Sun will eventually fade). It produces no pollution while generating electricity.

⚡ Fun Fact!

The amount of solar energy that hits Earth in just one hour is enough to power the entire world for a whole year! We just need to get better at capturing it all.

⚛️ Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is stored in the nucleus (center) of atoms. There are two ways to release it: fission (splitting large atoms apart) and fusion (joining small atoms together).

Nuclear power plants use fission — they split uranium or plutonium atoms, which releases enormous heat. That heat boils water into steam, which spins turbines to generate electricity.

Nuclear fusion is what powers the Sun! Scientists are working hard to build fusion power plants on Earth — fusion produces even more energy than fission and creates almost no waste.

⚡ Fun Fact!

A single uranium fuel pellet — about the size of a gummy bear — contains as much energy as 480 gallons of oil, 1 ton of coal, or 17,000 cubic feet of natural gas!

⚖️ Conservation of Energy

The Law of Conservation of Energy is one of the most important laws in all of science. It states that energy can never be created or destroyed — it can only be transformed from one form to another.

Think about riding a roller coaster. At the top, you have maximum potential energy and almost zero kinetic energy. As you zoom down, potential energy converts to kinetic energy. The total always stays the same!

When you eat food, chemical energy becomes kinetic energy (movement), thermal energy (body heat), and electrical energy (nerve signals). Even when energy seems to "disappear," it just transforms into heat, which spreads out into the environment.

⚡ Fun Fact!

This law was first properly stated by Julius Robert Mayer in 1842. Before that, scientists didn't fully understand that heat and motion were just different forms of the same thing!
